# Adopt the toolkit without the title

# 

 

For companies convinced of the framework but not yet hiring. Same instruments, lower political cost. Six months from now, you'll know whether to make the hire — and you'll have the data to justify it either way.

 

Printable: KPI baseline & target tracker.

 
 
 
 

If you scored 2‑3

 

Leadership‑alignment problem.

 

Someone in your room could own this. They're not, because no one asked them to. The first move is the mandate. Pick the most willing executive — usually your COO. Have a 30‑minute conversation this week. Use the mandate content in Block 02.

 
 
 

If you scored 4‑5

 

Hybrid path.

 

Pieces of this work are already happening — informally. The first move is to name and structure it. Pick the executive closest to the work, usually your COO. Have a 30‑minute conversation this week. Frame the mandate as clarification, not new assignment.

 
 

 
 
 01
 

## The shape of the move

## 
 
 

One existing executive — usually the COO — gets the friction‑removal mandate explicitly. They keep their current role. The mandate is additive: 20% of their time, with one strong technical PM as their execution arm.

 

The funding mechanic: 20% of the existing L&D budget, redirected. No new budget line. No new headcount above the technical PM. The mandate runs for six months, then you reassess against the value KPIs.

 

The structural commitment from you: the COO can convene any function head for review, can call for workflow rebuilds, can challenge headcount additions where AI substitution is possible. Disagreements with function heads come to you. That structural authority is the difference between this mandate working and dying in committee.

 

Before the mandate starts, you and the COO identify 2‑3 of their current responsibilities that pause, reassign, or de‑prioritize for six months. Write it down. Brief their direct reports so they don't escalate de‑prioritized items back. "20% time" without this conversation becomes "0% time absorbed by the day job" — and the mandate dies the first week of a sales emergency.

 
 
 02
 

## The mandate content

## 
 
 

What to communicate to your COO. Use this content in whatever form fits — email, written memo, Slack, in‑person conversation. The substance is what matters; the form is yours.

 
 
 Scope
 You own removing friction across IT, Human Capital, and Operations — the cross‑functional space where AI compresses cost and unlocks capacity. You don't replace those function heads; you push them to operate at the new ceiling.
 
 
 Authority
 You can convene any function head for review. You can call for workflow rebuilds. You can challenge headcount additions where AI substitution is possible. Disagreements with function heads come to me.
 
 
 Budget
 You operate within 20% of the existing L&D budget, redirected. No new budget line. If you need more, propose it with a measurable return.
 
 
 Time horizon
 Six months. We reassess at month six. If the value KPIs have moved, the mandate continues. If they haven't, we have a decision to make.
 
 
 Success measures
 Three numbers monthly: revenue per employee, cost per dollar of revenue, decision latency. Targets by month 6: revenue per employee up 15‑25%; cost per dollar of revenue down 5‑10%; decision latency cut in half for our top 20 recurring decisions.
 
 

 
 
 03
 

## The first four moves

## 
 
 

Four moves, in order. Each sized to the work, not the calendar.

 
 
 Step 1
 
 

Baseline the KPIs.

 

Your COO + finance pulls today's numbers for the seven metrics. Set targets. Print the tracker and fill it in. If any number is "we don't track this," that's the first one to start tracking. One to three days of work — don't move to Step 2 until this is done.

 
 
 
 Step 2
 
 

Staff the technical PM. The long pole.

 

Internal reassignment — preferred. One person you already have: strong technical taste, cross‑functional experience, comfort with structural conflict. Their existing work pauses or is covered by their team. Possible in days.

External hire — if no internal candidate. Expect 8‑12 weeks at a 500‑person company. Start the search the day you decide. The COO begins Step 3 alone in the meantime — the first workflow can be scoped without the PM, but the rebuild work waits for them.

 
 
 
 Step 3
 
 

Pick the first workflow.

 

The COO (with the PM once staffed) identifies one workflow currently running at 10% of what's possible. Pick one with high volume and clear ownership — easier to measure, harder to dispute when it improves. Avoid politically loaded workflows for the first rebuild. First wins should be unambiguous and unguarded.

 
 
 
 Step 4
 
 

Update the hiring rubric. Name the next three hires.

 

Add scoring for initiative, verification, taste, and feedback metabolism to all open roles. Brief your hiring panel. Then identify the next three hires that will use the new rubric — these become the leading indicator. Track agency‑weighted hires as Tier 3 of the KPI tracker. Not just a rubric update; a deliberate seeding of the workforce shift.

 
 
 

 
 
 04
 

## Months 2‑6 — Operate the cadence

## 
 
 

Three recurring rhythms. Each is short. Each is non‑negotiable.

 
 
 Monthly
 You + COO, 30 minutes. Review the three Tier 1 KPIs. Update the tracker. Discuss what shifted, what didn't, what's next. No deck. Numbers and decisions.
 
 
 Quarterly
 Full C‑suite, 60 minutes. COO presents what was rebuilt and what was learned — including what didn't work. Function heads volunteer their own gaps and the workflows they'd nominate for the next quarter. You listen. Decisions get made between meetings, not in them.
 
 
 Per workflow
 Rebuild → measure → decide. Each workflow rebuild gets a before/after measurement. If the rebuild produces the expected lift, the pattern gets replicated. If not, the team learns and picks the next one. No workflow rebuild runs longer than 90 days without a checkpoint.
 
 

 
 
 05
 

## Ceiling indicators

## 
 
 

At month 6, you'll be in one of two places. Both are useful.

 
 
 

Working

 
 
- Revenue per employee up 10% or more.
 
- Decision latency down meaningfully on the top 20 decisions.
 
- The COO is finding the next workflow without your prompting.
 
- Function heads are bringing AI‑substitution opportunities, not defending headcount.
 
- Your most agency‑weighted recent hires are already producing visible output.
 
 
 
 

Hitting limits

 
 
- The COO is asking for authority you didn't budget for.
 
- Workflow rebuilds are stalling in functions outside the COO's reach.
 
- Two or more function heads are openly resisting.
 
- The COO is at capacity — the side mandate is now eating their day job.
 
- KPIs moved but not by enough — the pace doesn't match the competitive window.
 
 
 

 

If you're in the left column, continue. The toolkit is working; extend the COO mandate to month 12 and pick the next set of workflows. If you're in the right column, the toolkit has hit its ceiling. You have the data to justify the executive hire — and the political case is now self‑evident to your board.
