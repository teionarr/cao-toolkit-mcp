# Your next hire isn't a Chief AI Officer. It's a Chief Agency Officer.

# 

 

Between 2010 and 2025, every domain company became an IT company. The next decade's winners will become AI‑native. Unless AI is your product, hiring another AI expert to lead that transition is the inertia that kills the move. The work isn't AI integration — it's a different shape of company.

## The role most companies hire — and why it fails

## 
 
 

The market saw the same problem. 76% of large organizations now have a Chief AI Officer in 2026, up from 26% a year earlier (IBM IBV 2026). The role exists. Companies are hiring it aggressively. And it's the wrong role.

 

A Chief AI Officer arrives as a policy and governance seat without operating authority — peer to function heads who don't report to them, dependent on those same function heads to implement what the CAIO recommends. The CAIO writes the AI strategy. The function heads decide whether to act on it. The systems get built. Then they get quietly sabotaged by every layer below — not out of malice, but out of self‑preservation. Every system that automates a function is a threat to the people who own that function.

 
 

What it looks like in practice

 
 
- Approvals get added.
 
- Edge cases get raised in committee.
 
- The rollout slows. The pilot stays a pilot for eighteen months.
 
- The CAIO calls it a "change management problem" and writes a deck about culture.
 
- The board nods. Nothing ships.
 
 

 

MIT Sloan and CIO Magazine independently predict the CAIO role will be absorbed back into existing functions within a few years as the technology matures. The market is hiring aggressively for a role the market itself will deprecate.

 

You have two choices. Bring a tech leader to build systems that replace people — viable if you're a new company, or an old one ready for a drastic shift. Or bring a leader who uncovers the human energy already inside the org, and fills every layer with people who use AI by default. The rest of this essay is about the second.

 
 
 

The 2025 instinct

 

Add a Chief AI Officer

 

Procures models. Deploys infrastructure. Writes the AI strategy. Lacks structural authority to make function heads adopt it. Spends months in committee. Becomes a presentation function.

 
 
 

The HR fallback move

 

Stretch your Human Capital Lead's mandate to AI

 

Optimizes for metrics they already own — retention, engagement, comp. AI becomes one more program inside a function built to steward human capital, not unlock human energy. Stewardship is not uncovering.

 
 
 

The 2026 move

 

Create a Chief Agency Officer

 

An executive who merges people, org, and tech. Makes the AI architecture and infrastructure calls through a human‑energy lens, not a systems lens. AI manages the company. People manage AI.

## The Chief Agency Officer

## 
 
 

So if not a Chief AI Officer, then what? The Chief Agency Officer is the vertical at the spine of an AI‑native company. Touches IT, Human Capital, and Operations the way Human Capital touches Engineering, Sales, and Ops — owns no function alone, shapes every function it crosses.

 

The goal isn't better tooling. The goal is people whose capacity to produce value grows 5x a year — not 5%. AI bends the curve from linear to exponential when the right executive removes what stops them.

 

The CTO builds the technology stack. The Human Capital Lead manages the people. The Chief Agency Officer removes what stands between them and value — by any combination of tooling, redesign, and subtraction.

 

Every era spawns vanity C‑suite titles. Most fade. The ones that survive own a measurable mandate no existing executive could absorb.

CFO

 
 
 

1970

CAO

 
 

 

Not Chief AI Officer. Not Chief Agentics Officer. Not Head of AI Transformation. Those are governance and literacy roles — real today, likely absorbed back into existing functions in 2‑3 years as the technology matures. AI is the substrate, not the subject. The word agency does double duty: agentic AI and human agency. Both grow together or neither grows.

 

Several institutions are pointing at this territory from different angles. None have named the synthesis.

This document calls it Chief Agency Officer because the word agency does what the others do with longer phrases. If you've already created a hybrid title doing this work — Chief Talent Alchemist, Chief Transformation Officer, your own naming — you're ahead of the curve. The principles here still apply; the convention is yours.

## The wrong org will eat the right people

## 
 
 

The CAO matters because the era has changed. To manage AI, you need high‑agency builders at every layer. You can start hiring them tomorrow. But without an advocate at the executive level, they walk into the same tenure‑weighted comp, the same approval layers, the same risk‑averse middle management. Six months in, they leave or stop being high‑agency. The era's prototype winner has changed; your org structure hasn't.

 
 
 

1850 — 1950

 

Physical capacity

 

The skilled operator

 

Strong hands, trained body, repeatable craft. Built and ran the physical economy.

 

Automation took the field.

 
 
 

1950 — 2020

 

Intelligence

 

The credentialed expert

 

The MBA, the engineer, the consultant, the analyst. Knew things others didn't. Knowledge was the wall.

 

The internet, then AI, took the field.

 
 
 

2025 — ?

 

Agency

 

The high‑agency builder

 

Picks up new domains in days. Ships before the brief is clean. Learns on real data within days. AI is their default tool, not a separate skill.

 

The next battleground for talent between companies.

 
 

 

Without the Human Capital Lead, people were a cost line. Without the Chief Agency Officer, AI is a software subscription.

## Agency is ten observable dimensions

## 
 
 

Agency is a new term — the kind we don't fully understand until five years after it's named. Remote work was that. The creator economy was that. Uberization was that. We'll be uncovering what "agency" means in business for years. We already know enough to start hiring for it, and to keep learning in the process.

 
 
 New · AI‑era
 

Verification

 

Knows when AI is confidently wrong.

 

The skill that separates force‑multipliers from slop‑amplifiers. Requires holding your own mental model strong enough to catch hallucinations, plausible‑but‑wrong reasoning, and confident nonsense.

 
 
 New · AI‑era
 

Taste

 

Recognizes good output without being told.

 

The aesthetic and judgment filter that stops infinite generation from collapsing into infinite mediocrity. Cannot be outsourced to the model. The scarcest input now.

 
 
 New · AI‑era
 

Question quality

 

Generative questions, not clarifying ones.

 

Clarifying questions extract; generative questions create new options. With AI, the prompt is the product — the person who asks "what if we approached this as X instead?" out‑leverages the person who asks "what format do you want?"

 
 
 New · AI‑era
 

Feedback metabolism

 

Asks for sharp feedback, updates loudly, changes behavior fast.

 

The learning loop. In an AI‑saturated environment, the people who iterate on themselves compound the fastest.

 
 
 Now critical
 

Initiative

 

Acts without being asked.

 

The willingness to be the first mover when no one has handed you the task. Pre‑AI and post‑AI, this is still the single most predictive trait.

 
 
 Now critical
 

Ambiguity tolerance

 

Moves before the brief is clean.

 

Starts with a v0.1 when the spec is 60% defined, knowing the act of building will clarify the rest. AI makes this cheaper, which rewards people who can tolerate the early fog.

 
 
 Now critical
 

Energy persistence

 

Pushes past the first plausible answer.

 

The first AI output is usually 70% there and feels finished. High‑agency people treat it as a starting draft, not a deliverable. This is grit relocated to the iteration layer.

 
 
 Now critical
 

Ownership

 

Says "I'll handle it" and the thing actually gets handled, end to end.

 

The classical dimension that AI doesn't replace — it amplifies. AI lets one owner do the work of five, but only if someone owns.

 
 
 Now critical
 

Follow‑through

 

Ships the boring last 30%.

 

AI is great at the exciting 70%; the gap between demo and shipped product is now almost entirely an agency tax.

 
 
 The outcome of all the above
 

Scope expansion

 

What they do at month 12 is meaningfully bigger than what they were hired for.

 

The residue of all the above. The clearest single signal that someone has agency, because it's the only one that can't be faked in an interview.

 
 

 

A high‑agency operator using AI as a default tool out‑ships a tenure‑weighted senior using AI as a faster Google. The market hasn't priced this. It will.

## Their field to fight

## 
 
 

Every company already addresses all six of these. Standups exist. Comp reviews happen. QA runs. Documentation gets filed. None of that is the problem.

 

The problem is that each one is being handled with pre‑AI methods — and AI now enables a 10x improvement on each. Not 10% better. An order of magnitude. The CAO is the executive who closes that gap, one obstacle at a time, while the rest of the org keeps running.

 
 
 

01

 

Coordination overhead

 

TodayWeekly syncs, status updates in three tools, cross‑team channels nobody reads in time.

 

10x versionAI digests every channel and surfaces only what someone needs to see, when they need to see it. Status meetings become async deltas.

 
 
 

02

 

Expertise ceiling

 

TodaySpecialists hold the knowledge. Everyone else waits for their meeting slot.

 

10x versionEvery person learns new domains on real company data in days, not years. Specialists become teachers and reviewers, not bottlenecks.

 
 
 

03

 

Verification overhead

 

TodayQA, code review, legal sign‑off, brand check. All manual, all serial, all slow.

 

10x versionVerification runs inline, automatically, against the company's own standards. Human review becomes the exception, not the default.

 
 
 

04

 

Unautomated repetition

 

TodayStatus reports written by hand. Onboarding run by humans. Reports rebuilt every quarter. Documentation drifts.

 

10x versionAnything done more than three times runs itself. People work on the part that's actually variable.

 
 
 

05

 

Context fragmentation

 

TodayInformation scattered across Slack, Notion, Linear, Salesforce, Drive, email, and three people's heads. The same question gets asked fortnightly.

 

10x versionAI assembles the answer from every system instantly. Knowledge stops being a thing you find and starts being a thing that finds you.

 
 
 

06

 

Incentive misalignment

 

TodayPromotion based on tenure‑weighted reviews, manager judgment, peer feedback rounds. Sincere effort; pre‑AI signal.

 

10x versionAI surfaces what each person actually shipped, against what was promised, at what value. Reviews become evidence‑based instead of memory‑based.

 
 

 

Every one of these is already a function in your company. Every one is being run at 10% of what's now possible. The CAO is the executive whose job is to close that gap — not by adding tools, but by rebuilding each function around what AI enables.

## A typical day

## 
 
 

The CAO doesn't do more. They work only at the edges of the company — where it's stuck, where it could go further. Company speed is set by its slowest function, not its average; the middle takes care of itself when the edges move. The lever throughout: AI built into the work itself, between idea and finished thing. Not a tool people open. A path the work runs on.

 
 
 Open
 

Observability sweep. Opens the analytics dashboard — top signals already filtered: opportunities, anomalies, risks. The system did the ranking overnight. One signal is worth acting on today.

 
 
 Ship
 

Prototype delivery. Top signal becomes a working prototype before lunch. Not by hand — a pipeline of agents handles it: one collects the data, one drafts the requirements, one builds, one tests. The CAO verifies at each step, redirects when wrong, ships when satisfied. The sales team gets the prototype with one line: "Try this for a sprint."

 
 
 Port
 

Software discipline to non‑tech. Walks the head of analytics through a CI/CD pipeline with evals for their new modules — the same shape engineering has run for a decade. Two weeks later, analytics ships three times the cadence with fewer regressions. The discipline didn't need to be invented; it needed to be moved.

 
 
 Route
 

Adoption diagnostic. A VP emails analytics asking "what's our churn by cohort?" — a question AI should have answered. The work isn't the answer, it's the pattern. By end of day, that VP's team is onboarded to the analytics‑AI layer. Questions of that shape stop reaching the desk.

 
 
 Judge
 

Vendor‑pick help. Product is choosing between two latency‑optimization vendors. They have AI; they could have asked anything. They didn't think to ask the model what they weren't asking. The CAO points that out, surfaces two questions the model identified — eval methodology under load, observability‑layer integration — and the decision becomes obvious in an afternoon.

 
 
 Synthesize
 

Shadow board. The CEO is weighing European expansion vs US enterprise. The CAO runs the question through a synthetic board of top operators in the relevant domain. Report in ten minutes — four cruxes, strongest counterargument to each path.

 
 
 Architect
 

Security session. Thirty minutes with the head of security. Not compliance — architecture. How do they build the next expertise bot, the verification automation, the cross‑system context fetcher without exposing the data perimeter? Security stops being the blocker on agentic projects and becomes the co‑designer.

 
 
 Close
 

Anomaly review + the one human conversation. Three deltas flagged: a team's shipped‑artifact rate dropped 40% (someone added an approval layer — removed by end of day), one individual is at 4x baseline (1:1 scheduled), one function's verification catch rate is climbing (win circulated). Then 45 minutes on the one thing that genuinely needed a human in the room.

 
 

 

The pattern across every segment: the system runs the work, the CAO runs the judgment. Not a busier executive. A different shape of one — operating where decisions still need a human, automated everywhere else.

## Who fits the chair

## 
 
 

You've seen the fight and the day. Now the shape of person who can run them.

 
 

A senior operator with builder's instinct, technical depth, and executive reach. Someone who has shipped at scale, can read an architecture diagram and a P&L with equal fluency, and has the political durability to fight for change without becoming a memo.

 
 

Most likely to come from one of five backgrounds:

 
 
- Ex‑COO of a fast‑scaled startup.
 
- Ex‑Chief of Staff at a frontier AI lab or top AI‑native company.
 
- Serial founder between companies.
 
- Technical leader whose second half of career bent outside pure software.
 
- Senior analytics leader whose work centered on changing how the org operated, not just measuring it.
 
 

Not a People Ops career path. Not a pure technologist. Not a consultant.

 
 
 

Operational modes

 
 
- Verifies a prototype pipeline.
 
- Redesigns an approval workflow.
 
- Argues a strategic crux in a board meeting.
 
 

Treats permission, incentive, headcount, and tooling as design surfaces — none of them as fixed.

 

 
 

Mindset

 

First‑principle thinking. Asks why a process exists before asking how to improve it. Most are vestigial; subtraction follows.

 

Systems over people. When something fails, the first question is what should have caught it automatically — not who dropped the ball.

 

Builds before deliberates. Ships a prototype that surfaces the answer instead of running a meeting that argues about it.

 

Asymmetrical bets. Every action is a bet. Reads the data on cost and expected value before spending an hour. Operates a portfolio, not a hunch.

 

Comfort with structural conflict. Companies are complex systems with intersecting interests; conflict isn't an event, it's the ambient condition. The CAO doesn't seek it, doesn't avoid it, isn't weighed down by it. They operate inside it.

 

High‑bandwidth attention. Twenty dashboards open, cross‑domain signal flowing in all day. The work is ever‑changing because the field is ever‑changing. The trait that survives this isn't curiosity — it's being undisturbed by the volume.

## KPIs that aren't theater

## 
 
 

A new function survives its first year only if it can be measured. Pick the wrong KPIs and the role becomes the very theater this document warns against. The test: a good KPI measures value created, not work performed.

 
 
 

Theater KPIs · avoid

 

 Hours of AI training delivered
 Prompts in the company library
 AI tools and agents deployed
 Workshops run
 Employee NPS on "AI experience"
 

 
 
 

Real KPIs · adopt

 

 Revenue per employee
 Gross margin per employee
 Growth rate of revenue per employee
 Cost per dollar of revenue
 Decision latency
 

 
 

 
 Tier 1 — Value KPIsThe headline numbers the owner reports to their board.
 
 
 

Revenue per employee

 

Revenue divided by FTE. The single cleanest measure of whether each person is producing more value. The owner already tracks this; the CAO is the executive whose work shows up here.

 

Direction: up. First movement in 6‑12 months; the order‑of‑magnitude shift takes 12‑24.

 
 6‑24 mo
 
 
 
 

Gross margin per employee

 

Revenue per employee net of cost to produce it. Captures the case where tooling spend rises while headcount holds — revenue per FTE might rise, but margin per FTE is the truer signal.

 

Direction: up. The CFO's preferred number.

 
 6‑12 mo
 
 
 
 

Growth rate of revenue per employee

 

Year‑over‑year acceleration in the headline number. The 5x‑a‑year claim from this document, measured directly. A growth rate, not a level.

 

Direction: up sharply. Sustained linear growth across multiple quarters means the role is failing.

 
 12‑24 mo
 

 Tier 2 — Velocity & costHow the value moved — through faster cycles or lower cost.
 
 
 

Cost per dollar of revenue

 

Total fully‑loaded cost — people, tools, vendors — divided by revenue. The operating ratio the CFO will fight over.

 

Direction: down. Falls faster than headcount, because revenue rises while headcount holds.

 
 6‑12 mo
 
 
 
 

Decision latency

 

Elapsed time from "decision needed" to "decision made" on tracked operational decisions. Slow cycles cap revenue growth. Supporting evidence: approval‑layer depth.

 

Direction: down. Falls measurably within two quarters if permission architecture is working.

 
 3‑6 mo
 

 Tier 3 — Leading indicatorsWhat moves first and predicts the value movement that follows.
 
 
 

Agency‑weighted hires

 

Percentage of new hires where agency‑dimension scores outweighed tenure‑years in the final decision. The earliest signal that the input pipeline is changing.

 

Direction: up. Direct measure of whether the hiring change is real or cosmetic.

 
 3‑12 mo
 
 
 
 

Tenure‑to‑impact correlation

 

Correlation between years‑at‑company and shipped value. The deep cultural signal — when this weakens, the value KPIs compound.

 

Direction: weakens. When agency drives value, the curve flattens.

 
 18‑36 mo
 
 

 

These metrics aren't new. What's new is their weight — in the AI‑native era, they decide who wins. And right now, no executive has the focus, the tools, and the mindset to push them. The CAO is the executive who does.

## Whose budget this eats

## 
 
 

A new C‑suite function only exists when it has territory. Here is where the territory comes from — with rough numbers, because vague claims are how new roles get strangled in their first year. IBM's 2026 CEO study found companies that rewired five areas at once — technology, finance, HR, operations, cross‑functional collaboration — were 4x more likely to meet their AI objectives than those that didn't. The CAO is the executive whose mandate spans those five.

 
 
 
 HR / People Ops
 
 ~20%
 
 

The hiring methodology slice — filters, performance review structure, calibration. Comp admin, benefits, compliance stay with HR.

 
 
 
 L & D
 
 ~60%
 
 

Most AI‑era expertise gets acquired on real projects, prototypes, and live processes — not in scheduled training. The 40% that remains is genuine domain skill‑building.

 
 
 
 IT / tooling
 
 20‑80%
 
 

Depends on what kind of company you are. If your main product is software, IT spend is product spend — the CAO eats only the slice that enables everyone else (~20%). If your main product is anything else — services, manufacturing, biotech, finance, consulting — IT is overhead and almost all of it becomes CAO territory (~80%).

 
 
 
 Middle management
 
 ~90%
 
 

The coordination overhead — meetings, status updates, cross‑team alignment — either automated or eliminated. Not the headcount: the function. Some managers transform into IC operators, some become CAO direct reports, the rest the company outgrows.

 
 

Each of these functions will fight to keep what it has. None of them can lead the work that takes it — no function restructures itself out of existence. Without an executive whose mandate sits above all four, this work doesn't happen. The CAO is that executive.

## Six hard questions

## 
 
 

The six objections this idea will face from a skeptical owner, board member, or existing C‑suite. Each one is real. None of them survive contact.

 
 
 

01Is this a technologist or a people leader? I can't tell which one to hire.

 

Neither, and both. The role isn't a technologist who happens to care about people; it's not a people leader who happens to use AI. It's a third shape — an executive whose primary tools are technical (agent pipelines, observability, evals, verification automation) and whose primary work product is structural (removing what stops people from producing more). The mindset to watch for: not controlling, pushing. Most C-suite roles control something — spend, headcount, operations, hiring. The CAO pushes — pushes the ceiling of what's possible, pushes the floor of what's stopping. If your candidate sounds like a governance officer, wrong shape.

 
 
 

02Agency can't be measured. This is pseudoscience dressed up in psychometrics.

 

The same objection was made about intelligence in 1905, leadership in 1950, and emotional intelligence in 1995. Each one resolved the same way: imperfect measurement that's better than no measurement. More importantly, the CAO doesn't lean on one instrument. The ten dimensions are one input; revenue per employee is another; decision latency is another. Every action is a data‑backed bet, triangulated across measurements. The alternative — "we can't measure agency so we'll keep hiring on tenure‑years" — is what got the company to the problem in the first place. And if the agency dimensions still feel soft, evaluate the CAO on the value KPIs alone — revenue per employee, cost per dollar of revenue, decision latency. Those numbers don't depend on psychometrics.

 
 
 

03If agency is so valuable, why doesn't the market already price it?

 

It does, badly. The market currently prices agency through founder equity and senior IC comp at frontier labs, which is why both compound so fast. In the broader enterprise market, 76% of large organizations now have a Chief AI Officer — up from 26% a year earlier (IBM IBV 2026). The market is hiring aggressively for the wrong shape. MIT Sloan and CIO Magazine independently predict the CAIO role will be absorbed back into existing functions within a few years as the technology matures. Supply for the right shape is small — most candidates already sit in high‑comp seats. By 2028, supply catches up through internal promotion and career pivots. The first companies to hire CAOs — who are also rare, also underpriced — will get them at a discount, and have years to build pipelines for the agency‑first technical talent the CAO attracts. The companies chasing in 2028 will pay three times the comp to attract half the quality — that's the arbitrage window, and it closes once the market catches on.

 
 
 

04You're describing a consultant engagement, not a permanent C‑suite role.

 

A consultant can audit your approval layers and recommend changes. They can't operate the systems that make this work — the observability layer, the agent pipelines, the evals catching AI errors in real time, the verification automation woven into legal and analytics and brand. They can't run a 24‑month series of data‑backed bets on which obstacle to remove next, watch the metrics, learn, redirect. They can't push limits — they recommend boundaries. The CAO is the executive whose job is to operate the system, not deliver findings about it. Consultants leave; the running system has to keep running.

 
 
 

05AI is the system. Why do I need an executive for what runs itself?

 

Today's systems don't run themselves yet — someone has to operate them. But that's the smaller answer. The deeper one: even when systems run more autonomously, someone has to decide which systems to build, which limits to push, which obstacles to attack first. Those are strategic calls, not technical ones. The CAO doesn't push the buttons. The CAO decides which buttons should exist.

 
 
 

06How do I know this is working in year one?

 

Most value KPIs take 12‑24 months. But three indicators move in year one and tell you whether the hire was right. Decision latency falls within two quarters — measurable. Agency‑weighted hires shows up in every hiring loop within 90 days. And the CAO themselves should be surfacing the evidence — that's part of the role. By month six, you should have a dashboard showing which obstacles were attacked, which moved, and which didn't. The qualitative test: are the company's top‑quartile people pushing for more responsibility, or quietly looking around? The CAO's first year is whether the floor moves. The ceiling moves in year two.

## The price of ignoring this

## 
 
 

You won't see this in your KPI dashboard first. You'll see it in the competitor your sales team keeps losing deals to — the one with 1/5 the headcount.

 
 
 Year 1
 
 

You notice the 1/5‑team competitor.

 

They're smaller, regional, easy to dismiss. But their revenue per employee is already 3x yours, and they're hiring faster. Your numbers haven't moved. The market's have. You'll attribute it to their stage, their luck, their niche — anything except the structural difference.

 
 
 
 Year 2
 
 

They're selling to your customers.

 

Pricing 30‑40% below yours and still profitable — because their cost per dollar of revenue is half. Your CFO blames the vendors. The real problem is that no one in your company operates the systems that would close the gap. You buy more tools; they pull more customers.

 
 
 
 Year 3
 
 

They're your size now.

 

Growing 3x faster, half the headcount, your best people on their team. Even if you hire a CAO today, the four functions that should have been compressed have hardened into structure. Your remaining team has been selected for compliance, not push. The restructure isn't expensive anymore — it's politically impossible.

 
 
 

 

The cost of hiring a Chief Agency Officer is one salary. The cost of not hiring one is the company.
